The Council’s ascent is vertiginous. By spring 2013 a South-Carolina senate seat opens after a mysterious car wreck, and Eugene floats his father’s name. Peter tastes genuine power—then Rosa leaks a tranche of Atlanta files linking the Rourke dynasty to a 1946 lynching and twenty-first-century zoning fraud. Donors bolt, Ray retreats, and Eugene abstains as the Council votes his father out. Peter is demoted to a windowless “consultant” cubicle, discovering too late that figureheads are cheap—and disposable.

Exile does not spare him the sight of what he has unleashed. From the Ferguson uprising to Donald Trump’s escalator descent, Peter watches echo after echo of his own slogans weaponised without attribution. Felicity flourishes as a law-and-order matriarch; Eugene perfects micro-targeting software that turns hurricane-relief rosters into voter-suppression gold. Online, Tony McQuade stitches Peter’s rhetoric into video manifestos that travel faster than truth. Peter feels pride and horror in equal measure: the movement no longer needs him, yet it still bears his fingerprints.

On Election Night 2016 the Restorationists claim the White House. Peter sits alone in his apartment, the television bleeding red states across the map, his phone silent. The last thing he burns before dawn is Rosa’s final dossier—proof that nothing stays buried forever, even in ash. He has achieved the title, proximity to power, and historic mention he craved, but at the cost of agency, family, and any remaining shard of conscience. He is, finally, the hollow patriarch of a dynasty that perfected his darkest impulses and left him behind.

**His Excellency** marries Southern-Gothic unease to contemporary political thriller, charting the rise of a manufactured elder-statesman and the personal collapse hidden inside public triumph. It is a story of dark money and darker nostalgia, of how fathers become scaffolds for more ruthless sons—and how scaffolds are meant to be kicked away.
